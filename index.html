Philadelphia · Asian Health Disparities Report

The data behind Asian health in Philadelphia

Asian Philadelphians live the longest of any racial group, yet face hidden disparities in mental health access, prenatal care, and a growing working-class population with real unmet need. This report synthesizes four data sources to tell the full story.

Chronic conditions

Low diagnosis rates — but are they real?

Asian adults show the lowest diagnosed chronic disease rates of any group. The question is how much reflects genuinely better health versus systematic underdiagnosis and underutilization of care.

Hypertension gap is a data quality flag. Philly's diagnosed hypertension for Asian adults is 3.1% — vs 22.9% in the national NHIS. This ~7× gap is implausibly large and almost certainly reflects systematic underdiagnosis or undercounting in the local survey, not a genuine 7-fold health advantage.
Smoking is the most reliable metric. Asian adults smoke at roughly half the White rate (9.6% vs 18.8% in Philly; 7.2% vs 15.5% nationally). This advantage is consistent across both data sources and likely genuine.

Depression is the strongest evidence of systematic underdiagnosis. The diagnosed depression rate (5–6%) is consistent across Philly and national data — two independent sources converging on the same implausibly low figure. The PHQ-8 objective screener narrows the gap considerably (2.3% Asian vs 7.3% White), but does not eliminate it, suggesting real population differences AND underdiagnosis are both at work.
Maternal, infant & child health

Mixed outcomes — one clear gap

Most birth outcomes are favorable for Asian Philadelphians. The one persistent gap: late or no prenatal care at nearly double the White rate, pointing to access barriers that persist despite generally better health status.

Philly Vital Statistics · Latest available year

Late prenatal care is the clearest access gap. 12.9% of Asian/PI mothers received late or no prenatal care — nearly double the White rate (6.75%) and close to the city average. This gap persists despite higher life expectancy, suggesting language access, insurance coverage patterns, and provider availability are barriers specific to pregnancy care.
Blood lead exposure is remarkably low. Only 0.46% of Asian/PI 3-year-olds tested positive for elevated blood lead — vs 7.6% White and 2.3% Black — likely reflecting newer housing and residential geography away from older lead-paint stock.
Cardiovascular disease

Population-adjusted: a genuine advantage, with one concern

Heart disease death rates for Asian/PI Philadelphians are roughly one-quarter the Black rate. But stroke mortality is rising and sits above the Hispanic rate — worth monitoring given elevated hemorrhagic stroke risk in East and South Asian populations.

Rising stroke mortality in Asian/PI residents needs monitoring. The stroke death rate went from 15.5/100k (2019) to 30.1/100k (2021) — doubling in two years. Even accounting for COVID-era volatility, East and South Asian populations globally have elevated hemorrhagic stroke risk linked to hypertension that is often undetected. This trend is consistent with the underdiagnosis of hypertension flagged earlier.

The "unhealthy assimilation" gradient. Asian immigrants who have been in the US 15+ years have hypertension rates of 31.7% — nearly triple recent arrivals (11.3%). Diabetes follows the same arc (13.2% vs 6%). This is one of the most robust findings in immigrant health research and should shape how Philadelphia targets preventive care for long-established Asian communities.
Geography · 18 planning districts

Where Asian Philadelphians live — and the social conditions there

Asian population share varies enormously across Philadelphia's 18 planning districts. Two distinct patterns: established, relatively affluent enclaves and fast-growing working-class neighborhoods with higher poverty and uninsurance.

Two very different Asian communities. South (6% poverty, 1.2% uninsured) and Central (11%, 2.8%) represent established, better-resourced enclaves. Central Northeast (18% poverty, 11% uninsured) and North Delaware (22%, 8%) — both with fast-growing Asian populations — are working-class neighborhoods with significantly higher need. A single "Asian health" policy approach misses this divide entirely.

Key Penn × community connection points
1
Jefferson × SEAMAAC → Penn Medicine
The Wyss Wellness Center proves Penn-adjacent health systems can partner with Asian-serving orgs at scale. Penn Medicine Family Care's multisite primary care network is a natural next partner for an equivalent model in South Philadelphia.
2
PASSi + Penn Medicine primary care
PASSi serves 800 seniors with chronic illness and is actively seeking an on-site primary care partner. Penn Medicine Geriatrics or Family Medicine is a direct fit — 85% of PASSi clients have conditions requiring daily treatment.
3
PennSmiles → South Philadelphia
The mobile clinic's planned South Philly expansion overlaps directly with SEAMAAC's new Snyder Ave HQ and PCDC's Chinatown footprint. A coordinated rollout would reach the densest Asian communities in the city.
4
Mental health gap × PCDC CIFWI
Asian depression diagnosis is 6.25% vs 29% for White adults — the starkest disparity in this report. PCDC's CIFWI + Wellness at Penn's multilingual counselors + Penn ASAM researchers is a natural research and service triangle.
5
Penn GSE × Moder Patshala
Penn GSE TESOL students already volunteer as tutors at Moder Patshala. This existing relationship can expand into health literacy, health navigation training, and parent engagement for Bangladeshi families.
6
CIR residents × AAPI patient populations
Penn Medicine's unionized residents explicitly prioritize health equity in underserved communities. Connecting CIR members to SEAMAAC, ACHC, and VietLead for community health rotations or care protocol co-design is actionable near-term.
